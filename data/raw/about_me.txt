Q: Explain your NER pipeline.
A: My NER pipeline processes financial SMS messages to extract structured information like transaction amount, merchant name, and transaction type. I start with text preprocessing, including tokenization and normalization. The tokens are then mapped to indices using a vocabulary built during training. These sequences are passed into a BiLSTM-CRF model, which captures both contextual dependencies and label sequence constraints. The output is a sequence of BIO tags, which I post-process to reconstruct meaningful entities like amounts and merchant names.
Q: How does your NER system work step by step?
A: First, I clean and tokenize the SMS text. Then I convert each token into an index based on a predefined vocabulary. These token sequences are fed into a BiLSTM layer to capture context from both directions. On top of that, I use a CRF layer to ensure valid tag sequences. Finally, I decode the predicted tags and map them back to entities like transaction amounts and merchants.
Q: What model architecture did you use for NER?
A: I used a BiLSTM-CRF architecture. The BiLSTM captures contextual relationships in the text, while the CRF layer ensures that the predicted tags follow valid sequence constraints, which improves overall accuracy.
Q: Why did you choose BiLSTM-CRF for NER?
A: I chose BiLSTM-CRF because it performs well on sequence labeling tasks. The BiLSTM captures context effectively, and the CRF layer helps enforce valid label transitions, which is important for structured outputs like named entities.
Q: What challenges did you face in building your NER pipeline?
A: One challenge was handling inconsistent SMS formats from different banks. Another was ensuring that the model generalized well despite limited labeled data. I addressed this by improving preprocessing and carefully designing the feature representation.

Q: Can you explain the BiLSTM-CRF architecture in detail?
A: The BiLSTM-CRF architecture combines a bidirectional LSTM with a Conditional Random Field layer. The BiLSTM processes the input sequence in both forward and backward directions to capture contextual dependencies. Its outputs are emission scores for each token. The CRF layer then models dependencies between adjacent tags and learns transition probabilities, ensuring that the final predicted sequence is globally optimal rather than making independent token-level decisions.
Q: Why is a CRF layer preferred over a softmax layer for NER?
A: A softmax layer predicts each token independently, which can lead to invalid tag sequences like an I-tag without a preceding B-tag. A CRF layer considers the entire sequence and enforces valid transitions, improving consistency and overall accuracy in structured prediction tasks.
Q: How does decoding work in a CRF layer?
A: Decoding in a CRF layer is typically done using the Viterbi algorithm, which finds the most likely sequence of tags by maximizing the total score, including both emission and transition probabilities.
Q: What loss function did you use for training your NER model?
A: I used the negative log-likelihood loss from the CRF layer, which maximizes the probability of the correct tag sequence over all possible sequences.
Q: How do you handle out-of-vocabulary words in your NER pipeline?
A: I map unknown tokens to a special <UNK> token. This ensures that unseen words at inference time can still be processed without breaking the pipeline.

Q: What sets you apart from other candidates?
A: My focus on end-to-end systems and on-device ML, along with hands-on deployment experience, sets me apart.
Q: Tell me about a failure in one of your ML systems.
A: One failure I encountered was inconsistent predictions between my Python training pipeline and Android inference. The root cause was differences in TF-IDF computation. I fixed it by exporting exact preprocessing parameters and reimplementing the logic precisely on Android, which taught me how critical pipeline consistency is.
Q: What is a trade-off you had to make in your system?
A: I had to trade off model accuracy for efficiency when deploying on-device. While transformer models performed better, I used BiLSTM-CRF because it provided a better balance between performance and resource constraints.
Q: When would you NOT use deep learning for a problem?
A: I would avoid deep learning when the dataset is small, the problem is simple, or interpretability is critical. In such cases, simpler models can be more efficient and easier to maintain.
Q: What is the biggest bottleneck in your ML pipeline?
A: The biggest bottleneck is usually preprocessing consistency and feature generation, not the model itself. Small inconsistencies can lead to large performance drops.
Q: How would you design a production LLM system?
A: I would design it with a retrieval layer, prompt management, caching, and monitoring. I would also ensure guardrails for hallucination control and optimize latency through batching or smaller models.
Q: What is retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)?
A: RAG combines a retrieval system with a language model so that the model generates responses based on relevant external documents instead of relying only on its internal knowledge.
Q: What problems does RAG solve?
A: It reduces hallucinations, improves factual accuracy, and allows models to work with up-to-date or domain-specific data.
Q: What are the challenges in building RAG systems?
A: Challenges include retrieval quality, context window limits, latency, and ensuring the retrieved data is actually useful for generation.
Q: How do you evaluate an LLM system?
A: I evaluate both qualitatively and quantitatively, using metrics like relevance, correctness, and consistency, along with human evaluation where needed.
Q: How would you design your expense tracker for 1 million users?
A: I would move heavy processing to scalable backend services, use distributed systems for ingestion, and implement caching and monitoring. I would also maintain on-device inference for latency-sensitive tasks.
Q: How do you handle data drift in production?
A: I monitor model performance over time, track input distributions, and retrain models periodically when drift is detected.
Q: What logging would you add to an ML system?
A: I would log inputs, predictions, confidence scores, and errors to help debug issues and monitor performance.
Q: How do you make ML systems reliable?
A: By adding validation checks, fallback mechanisms, monitoring, and ensuring reproducibility across environments.
Q: How would you optimize a large model for mobile deployment?
A: I would use quantization, pruning, knowledge distillation, and architecture simplification to reduce size and computation.
Q: What is quantization?
A: Quantization reduces the precision of model weights, typically from float32 to int8, to reduce memory usage and improve inference speed.
Q: How do you reduce latency in ML systems?
A: By optimizing preprocessing, using efficient models, caching results, and minimizing unnecessary computations.
Q: How do you improve a model without changing the architecture?
A: I improve data quality, add more training data, refine labels, and enhance feature engineering.
Q: What is more important: data or model?
A: Data is usually more important. A well-curated dataset can outperform complex models trained on poor data.
Q: How do you ensure label quality?
A: I define clear annotation guidelines, review samples, and iteratively refine labels based on errors.
